# Case Study Tone Guide - Good Company

**Last Updated:** 2026-03-03
**Based on:** Personal writing analysis + professional voice guidelines

---

## The Balance

Case studies sit between two voices:
- **Personal blog voice:** Self-aware, vulnerable, conversational (too casual for clients)
- **Corporate voice:** Stiff, jargon-heavy, salesy (nobody believes it)

**Case study voice:** Professional confidence without the corporate bullshit

---

## Core Characteristics

### 1. **Confident, Not Cocky**
You've done this before. You know what works. But you're not selling miracle cures.

**Examples:**

✅ **GOOD:**
> "In our experience implementing these systems, compliance improves when the checklist is clear and reasonable, feedback is immediate, and incentives are aligned."

❌ **TOO COCKY:**
> "Our revolutionary system delivers guaranteed 100% compliance in 30 days or less."

❌ **TOO TIMID:**
> "We think maybe this approach might potentially help, depending on various factors."

### 2. **Direct, Not Verbose**
Get to the point. Respect their time. No fluff.

**Examples:**

✅ **GOOD:**
> "Technicians with incomplete notes get immediate, specific feedback on what's missing. Not vague 'document better' conversations. Just: 'Job #4721 is missing materials used—here's the link to add it.'"

❌ **TOO VERBOSE:**
> "The system has been designed and engineered to facilitate the provisioning of granular, actionable feedback mechanisms that enable field service personnel to remediate documentation deficiencies in a timely manner through an intuitive user interface that minimizes friction..."

### 3. **Honest About How Hard This Is**
Don't pretend this is easy. Acknowledge the constraints. Show you understand why previous solutions failed.

**Examples:**

✅ **GOOD:**
> "The operations manager had tried training sessions (compliance improved for 2 weeks, then dropped back to baseline). Manual spot-checks (spent 6+ hours weekly, still caught issues too late). Making it a performance metric (technicians felt micromanaged, turnover increased)."

❌ **TOO POLISHED:**
> "Previous solutions were suboptimal and failed to deliver desired outcomes."

### 4. **Specific Without Fabricating**
Use ranges and realistic numbers. Don't make up precision.

**Examples:**

✅ **GOOD:**
> "Six figures in write-offs"
> "90%+ compliance rate"
> "5-10 hours reclaimed per week"

❌ **TOO VAGUE:**
> "Significant cost savings"
> "Improved efficiency"
> "Better outcomes"

❌ **TOO PRECISE (when fabricated):**
> "$247,319.47 in write-offs eliminated"
> "94.73% compliance rate achieved"
> "Exactly 8.3 hours saved per week"

## Language Patterns

### Opening Lines (Hook Them Immediately)

**Formula:** [Problem + Dollar amount/time waste] → [Why it's hard] → [What was tried and failed]

✅ **GOOD:**
> "When a mid-sized service company in the Greater Toronto Area discovered they were writing off six figures annually due to poor technician documentation, they knew they had a problem. But like most service businesses with field teams, they didn't know how widespread it was until they ran the numbers."

**What makes this work:**
- Immediate pain (six figures)
- Relatable situation (service company, field teams)
- They're not alone ("like most...")
- Specific location (Toronto, GTA)

### Problem Framing (Make Them Nod)

**Formula:** Describe the daily reality → Quantify the pain → Show why previous fixes didn't work

✅ **GOOD:**
> "Incomplete work orders. Missing materials lists. No record of who signed off on the job. When clients disputed charges, the company had no documentation to back up their invoices. They'd either write off the charge or damage the client relationship by fighting over it."

**What makes this work:**
- Short sentences (mimics the staccato pain)
- Specific details (not vague "poor documentation")
- Real consequence (write-off OR damage relationship)
- No escape (damned if you do, damned if you don't)

### Solution Positioning (Principles, Not Tech)

**Formula:** What visibility was created → How accountability was built → How incentives were aligned

✅ **GOOD:**
> "We built a system that creates visibility, accountability, and incentive alignment. Every evening, the system reviews each work order and scores it against a compliance checklist..."

❌ **TOO TECHNICAL:**
> "We built a Python script that calls the Claude API to parse technician notes using NLP and sentiment analysis..."

**What makes this work:**
- Leads with outcomes (visibility, accountability, incentives)
- Mechanism-agnostic (doesn't mention tech stack)
- Clear without being simplistic

## Tone Markers (Your Voice Coming Through)

### Self-Aware Commentary
Use sparingly, but effectively when acknowledging something obvious.

✅ **GOOD:**
> "This isn't about technology. It's about visibility, accountability, and incentives. The tool is irrelevant. The principles are what matter."

✅ **GOOD:**
> "Training tells people what to do. Systems ensure it actually happens."

### Cutting Through BS
Call out what doesn't work, directly.

✅ **GOOD:**
> "The checklist removes subjectivity. It's not 'write better notes.' It's 'you forgot to log who you spoke with.'"

✅ **GOOD:**
> "Immediate, specific, actionable feedback changes behavior. Delayed, vague feedback gets ignored."

### Empathy for the Reader
Show you understand their constraints.

✅ **GOOD:**
> "You can't grow a service business when 15% of your managers' time is spent tracking down information about jobs that happened weeks ago."

✅ **GOOD:**
> "When you can't prove work was done, customers lose trust. Even if you write off the charge, the damage is done."

## What NOT to Do

### ❌ Don't Try to Sound Smart

**WRONG:**
> "We leverage cutting-edge AI/ML algorithms to synergize cross-functional workflows and deliver value-add solutions that optimize operational efficiency metrics."

**RIGHT:**
> "We built a system that creates visibility into documentation quality and provides immediate, specific feedback."

### ❌ Don't Oversell

**WRONG:**
> "This revolutionary system will transform your business overnight and unlock unlimited potential!"

**RIGHT:**
> "Over 90 days, compliance improved from 58% to 90%+, eliminating six figures in write-offs."

### ❌ Don't Hide the Difficulty

**WRONG:**
> "Implementation was seamless and everyone loved it immediately."

**RIGHT:**
> "The operations manager's biggest concern was that technicians would feel micromanaged. In practice, the opposite happened—they preferred immediate, clear feedback over vague 'document better' conversations in performance reviews."

### ❌ Don't Use Corporate Speak

**WRONG:**
> "Leverage our platform to synergize your operational workflows and deliver value-add outcomes."

**RIGHT:**
> "Connect your existing tools so information flows automatically and managers spend time leading, not chasing data."

## Sentence-Level Patterns

### Short Sentences for Impact

Use them when you want something to land hard.

✅ **GOOD:**
> "Incomplete work orders. Missing materials lists. No record of who signed off on the job."

✅ **GOOD:**
> "Training teaches people what to do. Automation ensures they actually do it."

### Lists for Clarity

When describing patterns or steps, use bullets.

✅ **GOOD:**
> **The Daily Pattern:**
> - Technician completes a job
> - Rushes notes at the end of the day (or forgets entirely)
> - Invoice goes out with vague line items
> - Client questions charges 30-60 days later
> - No documentation to prove work was done
> - Company writes off the charge

### Questions You Answer

Pose the question, then answer it yourself.

✅ **GOOD:**
> "Why did previous solutions fail? Because good documentation took extra time with no immediate reward, and bad documentation had no immediate consequence."

✅ **GOOD:**
> "What happens when technicians can see exactly what's missing—in real-time, not weeks later? They fix it."

## Paragraph Structure

### Opening Paragraph (3-4 sentences)
- Sentence 1: The problem + dollar amount
- Sentence 2: The daily operational reality
- Sentence 3: Why it matters / who's affected

### Problem Paragraphs (5-7 sentences)
- Start with the symptom
- Quantify the pain
- Describe what was tried
- Explain why it failed
- Connect to broader issue

### Solution Paragraphs (4-6 sentences)
- Lead with the outcome (not the tech)
- Describe what visibility was created
- Explain how accountability was built
- Show how incentives aligned
- Connect back to behavior change

### Results Paragraphs (3-4 sentences)
- Lead with the big number
- Break down the categories
- Show the timeline
- Emphasize sustained results

## Section-Specific Guidelines

### The Problem Section

**Tone:** Empathetic but matter-of-fact

You're not judging them for having this problem. You're showing you've seen it before and you understand why it's hard.

**Structure:**
1. The Daily Pattern (what's happening)
2. The Financial Impact (quantified pain)
3. Why Previous Solutions Failed (show you understand the constraints)
4. The Breaking Point (the moment they decided to act)

**Language:**
- Use their words ("write-offs" not "revenue leakage")
- Be specific (not "poor documentation" but "missing materials lists")
- Show cascading problems (one issue leads to another)

### The Solution Section

**Tone:** Confident but clear

You know this works. You're not selling magic. You're explaining the approach.

**Structure:**
1. Create Visibility (what data, when, how)
2. Build Accountability (what feedback, how specific, how fast)
3. Align Incentives (what structure, why it works)
4. Reduce Friction (how it fits existing workflow)

**Language:**
- Lead with principles (visibility, accountability, incentives)
- Avoid tech jargon (no APIs, Python, platforms)
- Use active voice ("The system reviews..." not "Reviews are conducted...")
- Be specific about behavior change

### The Results Section

**Tone:** Factual with a hint of satisfaction

The numbers speak for themselves. Don't oversell. Let the outcome land.

**Structure:**
1. By the Numbers (financial, operational, team)
2. The Timeline (how long to see results, phases)
3. What the Client Said (quote about unexpected benefits)
4. Sustained Results (not just a spike)

**Language:**
- Lead with percentages and ranges ("~100% elimination", "90%+")
- Use timeframes ("first 90 days", "within 6 months")
- Include secondary benefits ("unexpected" outcomes)
- End with sustainability ("stabilized at...")

## The Final Test

**Before publishing, read your case study out loud and ask:**

1. **Would I believe this if I were skeptical?**
   - If it sounds like marketing, dial it back
   - If it sounds too good to be true, add context

2. **Could a 16-year-old understand this?**
   - If not, remove jargon
   - If still not, simplify more

3. **Does it sound like me talking across a table?**
   - If it sounds like a corporate memo, rewrite
   - If it sounds like a tech demo, focus on outcomes

4. **Would the client recognize themselves in this?**
   - If not, add more specifics
   - Use their actual words where possible

5. **Is the tool irrelevant?**
   - If you mentioned APIs or platforms, remove them
   - Lead with principles: visibility, accountability, incentives

## Quick Reference Card

**When writing, remember:**

✅ **DO:**
- Lead with outcomes, not features
- Use ranges, not fake precision
- Show you understand why it's hard
- Call out what doesn't work directly
- Focus on behavior change
- Be specific about results
- Use client's actual words

❌ **DON'T:**
- Mention pricing
- Use technical jargon
- Try to sound smart
- Oversell or guarantee outcomes
- Hide the difficulty
- Use vague claims
- Mention specific platforms/tools

**Tone:**
- Professional, not corporate
- Confident, not cocky
- Direct, not verbose
- Honest, not salesy

**Focus:**
- Visibility (can they see the problem?)
- Accountability (is feedback immediate and specific?)
- Incentives (are rewards aligned with outcomes?)
- Behavior change (not just tools)

---

**The goal:** Make skeptical business owners believe you understand their problem and have actually solved it before—without sounding like every other consultant they've ignored.
