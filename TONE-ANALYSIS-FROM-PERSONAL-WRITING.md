# Tone Analysis: Specific Principles from Personal Writing

**Last Updated:** 2026-03-03
**Sources:** 7+ personal blog posts analyzed for patterns

---

## Meta-Principle: Write Like You're Admitting Something

The most consistent pattern across all your writing: **you're always admitting to something**—a failure, a misconception, something embarrassing, or something you got wrong.

This creates instant credibility because you're not positioning yourself as the expert who has it all figured out. You're the person who fucked up and learned something.

---

## Pattern #1: The Vulnerable Opening Hook

### From "Brick by Brick" (143314587):

**Opening:**
> "Maybe you know the feeling:
> *Thoughts all over the place…
> trouble sticking to a consistent plan…
> maybe even a little lost.*
>
> I definitely do."

**What makes this work:**
- Starts with empathy ("Maybe you know the feeling")
- Uses italics for emphasis and rhythm
- Short, punchy fragments (not complete sentences)
- Immediately admits vulnerability ("I definitely do")
- No preamble, no setup—straight to the feeling

**How to translate to case studies:**

❌ **CORPORATE:**
> "Service businesses often face documentation challenges that impact their operational efficiency."

✅ **YOUR VOICE:**
> "Maybe you know the feeling: Your technicians are doing great work. But when clients dispute charges weeks later, you have no documentation to prove it. You write off the charge or damage the relationship fighting over it.
>
> Most service companies deal with this daily."

## Pattern #2: Self-Deprecating Parenthetical Commentary

### From Multiple Articles:

**"Brick by Brick":**
> "(ie. a very high level)"
> "(…but aren't we all?)"
> "*(I hate that I just said this…)*"

**"You Can Do Whatever You Want":**
> "(*Honestly, who doesn't?*)"
> "*(So long as I keep bringing in revenue.)*"

**"Stop Using Acronyms":**
> "(I don't care about or like politics)"

**What makes this work:**
- Breaks the fourth wall
- Shows self-awareness
- Acknowledges when you're being cliché or annoying
- Makes the reader feel like you're talking directly to them
- Creates authenticity (you're not trying to sound smart)

**How to translate to case studies:**

❌ **CORPORATE:**
> "The implementation timeline varies based on organizational complexity and stakeholder alignment."

✅ **YOUR VOICE:**
> "Most companies see results in 90 days. Some faster, some slower. It depends on how quickly your team adopts it. (And whether everyone actually uses it—spoiler: that's the hard part.)"

## Pattern #3: The "Painful Truth" Confession

### From "Brick by Brick":

**The Setup:**
> "4 months ago, I watched an Alex Hormozi video and was embarrassed when a few points during *'what not to do'* resonated a little too loudly with how 2021 felt - **particularly a piece about jumping from thing to thing.**"

**The Painful Admission:**
> "It's painful to acknowledge now in 2024 that any one of those ideas COULD have worked - they only died because I felt the need for them to be instantly successful.
>
> And the need for them to be instantly successful meant NONE of them became successful at all.
>
> ***Ouch.***"

**What makes this work:**
- Uses "embarrassed" (vulnerability)
- Admits the thing that hurts to admit
- One-word paragraph for impact ("***Ouch.***")
- Doesn't shy away from the failure
- Makes it relatable (everyone wants instant success)

**How to translate to case studies:**

❌ **CORPORATE:**
> "Previous solutions were suboptimal due to inadequate implementation strategies."

✅ **YOUR VOICE:**
> "The operations manager had tried training sessions. They worked for two weeks. Then compliance dropped right back to where it started.
>
> Manual spot-checks? Spent 6+ hours weekly reviewing notes. Still caught issues too late.
>
> Making it a performance metric? Technicians felt micromanaged. Turnover went up.
>
> The problem wasn't effort. It was approach."

## Pattern #4: The "Let's Be Clear" Direct Statement

### From Multiple Articles:

**"Brick by Brick":**
> "I didn't need to actively practice patience, because I had zero expectations of what the outcome should be."

**"Shamelessness is a Superpower":**
> "The shameless person is so busy doing…
> that they don't have time to think about…
> what you might think about them…
> doing the thing that they are doing…"

**What makes this work:**
- No hedging ("maybe", "perhaps", "possibly")
- States the insight directly
- Uses rhythm and repetition
- Breaks conventional sentence structure for emphasis

**How to translate to case studies:**

❌ **CORPORATE:**
> "Organizations may potentially experience improved outcomes when implementing systematic feedback mechanisms."

✅ **YOUR VOICE:**
> "Training tells people what to do. Systems ensure they actually do it.
>
> The difference matters more than any amount of jargon."

## Pattern #5: The List That Builds Rhythm

### From "Brick by Brick":

**Setup:**
> "If you've been reading long enough, you likely know what I am referring to:
>
> I've started businesses,
> stopped businesses,
> gotten excited about business ideas,
> lost excitement about business ideas,
> gotten existential about business ideas,
> gotten frustrated about business ideas,
> forgotten about business ideas…"

**Another Example:**
> "I had forgotten how I even developed those successes at all - **one day at a time.**
>
> One email at a time.
> One event at a time.
> One brand partnership at a time.
> One client at a time.
> One great story at a time."

**What makes this work:**
- Repetitive structure creates rhythm
- Each line is parallel construction
- Builds momentum
- The ellipsis at the end suggests "and on and on..."
- Makes the point viscerally felt, not just stated

**How to translate to case studies:**

❌ **CORPORATE:**
> "The company experienced multiple categories of revenue loss due to insufficient documentation practices."

✅ **YOUR VOICE:**
> "Incomplete work orders.
> Missing materials lists.
> No record of who signed off.
>
> When clients disputed charges, the company had nothing. They'd either write off the charge or damage the relationship fighting over it."

## Pattern #6: Calling Out Your Own Bullshit

### From "Brick by Brick":

**On Clichés:**
> "Over the years, I've had more than a few mentors repeat irritating cliches like:
> - *Trust the process…*
> - *It's about the journey, not the destination…*
> - *It's a marathon, not a sprint…*
>
> *Blah blah blah…*I hated hearing it.
>
> But one of two possibilities is true:
> A) All my mentors lacked imagination and used the same tired cliches;
> OR
> **B) Perhaps they all knew it was something I desperately needed to hear.**"

**Then Later:**
> "So here I am, publicly criticizing myself in order to make a point:
> - *Slow is smooth and smooth is fast…*
> - *Consistency is key…*
> - *Rome wasn't built in a day…*
>
> It's a piss off, but the cliches are true."

**What makes this work:**
- Acknowledges the clichés are annoying
- Admits he hated them
- Then admits they're actually true
- The self-awareness makes the clichés land harder
- "It's a piss off, but..." = frustrated acceptance

**How to translate to case studies:**

❌ **CORPORATE:**
> "Sustainable behavior change requires consistent reinforcement mechanisms."

✅ **YOUR VOICE:**
> "You've heard it before: 'What gets measured gets managed.'
>
> It's one of those business clichés that makes you roll your eyes. Until you try to change behavior without measuring it.
>
> Then you realize the cliché exists for a reason."

## Pattern #7: The Analogy That Lands

### From "Brick by Brick":

**The Tree Analogy:**
> "To use a lame analogy - if you planted a tree, would you expect it to be 30 feet tall overnight?
>
> Probably not…
>
> So why do we expect any different in business?"

**What makes this work:**
- Calls it "lame" (self-aware)
- Uses simple, relatable comparison
- Rhetorical question
- Pause after "Probably not..."
- Connects back to the point directly

**How to translate to case studies:**

❌ **CORPORATE:**
> "Behavioral transformation requires temporal investment and sustained commitment."

✅ **YOUR VOICE:**
> "Think about it: When you hire someone, you don't expect them to be fully productive on day one. You give them 90 days to ramp up.
>
> Why would a new system be any different?"

## Pattern #8: The "Here's What Actually Happened" Story Arc

### From "Brick by Brick":

**The Full Arc:**

1. **Admission:** "In 2021, I was searching for what to do next, but couldn't come up with the right answer."

2. **The Failure:** "None of my ideas were being executed to the level I believe they should be."

3. **The Wake-Up Call:** "I watched an Alex Hormozi video and was embarrassed..."

4. **The Insight:** "I had forgotten how I even developed those successes at all - one day at a time."

5. **The Lesson:** "Good things take time to compound."

**What makes this work:**
- Chronological narrative
- Specific details (Alex Hormozi video, 2021, etc.)
- Vulnerable throughout
- Clear turning point
- Actionable insight at the end

**How to translate to case studies:**

❌ **CORPORATE:**
> "After evaluating multiple intervention strategies, the organization implemented a comprehensive solution framework."

✅ **YOUR VOICE:**
> "The operations manager had tried everything. Training sessions that didn't stick. Manual reviews that ate up hours. Performance metrics that killed morale.
>
> Nothing worked.
>
> The problem wasn't effort. It was that all those solutions focused on catching problems after the fact. Too late to do anything about them.
>
> That's when they realized: What if technicians got feedback the same day? Before it became a write-off. Before it became a problem."

### Examples Across Articles:

**"Brick by Brick":**

>
> "**This is a costly thing to forget.**"
>
> "**It cannot be overstated how hugely important this was.**"

**"Shamelessness is a Superpower":**
> "**Everyone's got a different reason.**"

**"You Can Do Whatever You Want":**
> "Just space."

**What makes this work:**
- Forces the reader to pause
- Creates emphasis without shouting
- Makes the point land harder
- Shows you're not rushing
- Breaks up dense paragraphs

**How to translate to case studies:**

❌ **CORPORATE:**
> "This represents a significant operational improvement with measurable financial impact across multiple performance dimensions."

✅ **YOUR VOICE:**
> "Six figures in write-offs. Gone.
>
> That's the kind of number that gets your attention."

## Pattern #10: The Callback (Referencing Earlier Points)

### From "Brick by Brick":

**Early in article:**
> "It didn't feel like a 'start-up' phase because I had zero expectations of what the outcome should be."

**Later callback:**
> "And because of this, I wasn't jumping from idea to idea, landing in the 'Valley of Despair.'"

**Final callback:**
> "I had forgotten how I even developed those successes at all - **one day at a time.**"

**What makes this work:**
- Creates cohesion across the piece
- Reinforces key concepts
- Shows you're building an argument, not just listing points
- Makes it feel like a conversation, not a lecture

**How to translate to case studies:**

❌ **CORPORATE:**
> [No callbacks, just linear progression of points]

✅ **YOUR VOICE:**
> **Early:** "Technicians didn't see documentation as a priority. It wasn't that they didn't care—it took extra time with no immediate reward."
>
> **Later:** "That's why immediate feedback worked. Remember—the problem wasn't effort. It was that good documentation had no reward and bad documentation had no immediate consequence."

## Pattern #11: The "Stop Doing This" Imperative

### From "Stop Using Acronyms":

**The Direct Commands:**
> "Stop using your stupid acronyms
>
> Stop using bloated terms.
>
> Stop trying to be smart.
>
> The smarter you're trying to be, the stupider you sound."

**What makes this work:**
- Imperative form (commands, not suggestions)
- Repetitive structure
- Builds to the punch line
- No hedging or softening
- Direct, almost aggressive

**How to translate to case studies:**

❌ **CORPORATE:**
> "Organizations should consider optimizing their communication frameworks to enhance stakeholder engagement."

✅ **YOUR VOICE:**
> "Stop sending technicians vague feedback like 'document better.'
>
> Start telling them exactly what's missing: 'Job #4721 has no materials logged. Here's the link to add them.'
>
> The difference is everything."

**What makes this work:**

## Pattern #13: The Unexpected Detail That Makes It Real

### From "Brick by Brick":

**The Cash in Shoe Boxes:**
> "We were making so much money, I kept it hidden in shoe boxes or sock drawers - and as my roommate can attest to, often had it fall out of my jeans that I left laying around the house."

**The Facebook Memory:**
> "This FB memory popped up the other day - an altercation I had with a prof that I posted about in 2011…The Red Bull Threestyle would've been that week - which I won - and given that I don't remember the paper at all, it was probably not important."

**What makes this work:**
- Hyper-specific detail
- Weird enough to be memorable
- Humanizes the story
- Can't be made up (too specific)
- Makes the reader go "oh shit, this is real"

**How to translate to case studies:**

❌ **CORPORATE:**
> "The operations manager experienced significant time savings."

✅ **YOUR VOICE:**
> "The operations manager used to spend Thursday afternoons reviewing notes from the previous week. By the time she caught an issue, the job was done weeks ago. Now? The system catches it the same day. Thursday afternoons are for strategy, not documentation police work."

## Pattern #14: The Scene-Setting Opening

### From "You Can Do Whatever You Want":

**Full Opening:**
> "We were at an early breakfast downtown in 2015.
>
> It was cold and raining — the kind of morning that makes the city feel hushed, where the best place to be is tucked in the corner of a coffee shop, letting the day unfold however it wants to.
>
> For me, it was one of those slow, quiet mornings where the coffee hits just right and the conversation could stretch into late morning.
>
> For my friend, it was the opposite — she had a hard stop."

**What makes this work:**
- Specific time and place (2015, early breakfast, downtown)
- Sensory details (cold, raining, hushed city)
- Sets up contrast (you vs. friend)
- No rush to get to the point
- Lets the scene breathe

**How to translate to case studies:**

❌ **CORPORATE:**
> "A service company faced operational challenges related to field documentation."

✅ **YOUR VOICE:**
> "The operations manager knew something was wrong when she spent an entire Friday morning chasing down information about a job that happened three weeks ago. The client was disputing a $3,000 charge. The technician couldn't remember the details. The invoice said 'labor and materials.' That was it.
>
> She wrote off the charge. Again."

## Summary: Core Voice Principles for Case Studies

### 1. **Lead with Admission, Not Authority**
Start by admitting the client was stuck, not by claiming you're the expert.

### 2. **Use Parenthetical Self-Awareness**
(And call out when you're being cliché or annoying.)

### 3. **Short Sentences for Impact**
Not everything needs to be. A full grammatically correct sentence. Sometimes fragments land harder.

### 4. **Build Rhythm with Repetition**
Same structure, multiple lines. It compounds.

### 5. **Call Out Your Own Clichés**
Then admit they're true anyway. It works.

### 6. **Hyper-Specific Details**
Not "saved time." "Went from spending Thursday afternoons reviewing notes to spending 20 minutes reviewing a weekly summary."

### 7. **One-Sentence Paragraphs**
For emphasis.

### 8. **Analogies (Even Lame Ones)**
"To use a lame analogy..." then use it anyway.

### 9. **The Painful Truth Section**
Always include the part that hurts to admit.

### 10. **Stop Trying to Sound Smart**
The smarter you try to sound, the stupider it reads.

## The Final Test (Your Voice Edition)

Before publishing a case study, ask:

1. **Would I actually say this across a table?**
   - If it sounds like a LinkedIn post, rewrite it.

2. **Did I admit something painful?**
   - If not, find the admission. It's what makes it credible.

3. **Am I trying to sound smart?**
   - If yes, you're doing it wrong. Simplify.

4. **Could a skeptical person believe this?**
   - If not, add more specific details or tone down the claims.

5. **Is there at least one "Ouch" moment?**
   - The moment where the reader goes "oh shit, that's real."

---

**The pattern across all your writing:** You're always telling the reader something you learned the hard way. Not teaching down to them. Not positioning yourself as the guru. Just: "Here's what I fucked up, here's what I learned, maybe it helps you."

That's the voice. That's what makes it work.
