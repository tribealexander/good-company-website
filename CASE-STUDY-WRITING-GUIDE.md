# Case Study Writing Guide - Good Company

**Last Updated:** 2026-03-03

---

## Purpose of This Guide

Case studies serve two purposes:
1. **Trust-building** - Prove you've done this before and it worked
2. **Traffic generation** - Rank for problem-focused keywords and bring in organic leads

This guide ensures every case study is consistent in tone, structure, and messaging.

---

## Core Principles (Never Compromise These)

### 1. **Principles Over Tools**
- ❌ DON'T talk about: Python scripts, API calls, specific platforms, GitHub Actions, technical implementation
- ✅ DO talk about: Visibility, accountability, incentives, change management, behavior change
- **Why:** The tool is irrelevant. The approach is what matters. Business owners don't care about the tech stack—they care about the outcome.

**Example (WRONG):**
> "We built a Python script that calls the Claude API to assess note quality..."

**Example (RIGHT):**
> "We built a system that creates visibility into documentation quality and provides immediate, specific feedback..."

### 2. **Value Over Features**
- ❌ DON'T lead with: "We integrated with their FSM software using API endpoints..."
- ✅ DO lead with: "Operations managers were spending 6+ hours weekly chasing down information..."
- **Why:** Business owners buy outcomes, not integrations.

### 3. **Specificity Without Fabrication**
- ✅ Use ranges, not exact numbers (unless verified by client)
- ✅ "Six figures in write-offs" not "$243,719 in write-offs"
- ✅ "90%+ compliance" not "94.7% compliance"
- ❌ Never mention pricing for engagements (no "$8K-$15K implementation")
- **Why:** Credibility through honesty. Specificity builds trust, but fake precision destroys it.

### 4. **Change Management Over Automation**
- Focus on: How behavior changed, how team dynamics shifted, how incentives aligned
- Not on: How the system works technically
- **Why:** Automation projects fail because of change management, not technical issues.

## Tone & Voice

### Target Audience
**Who:** Business owners, operators, VPs/Directors of Operations
- Mid-market businesses ($1M-$50M revenue)
- 10-200 employees
- Toronto/GTA primarily, but applicable broadly
- Skeptical of consultants and technology promises
- Have been burned before by "solutions" that didn't stick

**What they care about:**
- Will this work in MY business?
- Will my team actually use it?
- How long until I see results?
- What happens when you leave?

### Voice Characteristics

**Professional, not corporate:**
- Write like you're talking to someone across a boardroom table
- Confident, not cocky
- Direct, not verbose
- Consultative, not salesy

**Clear, not clever:**
- No jargon or acronyms (FSM, ROI, KPI) unless absolutely necessary
- No buzzwords ("synergy", "leverage", "utilize")
- Simple, specific language
- If a 16-year-old can't understand it, simplify it

**Honest, not salesy:**
- Acknowledge limitations
- Don't oversell ("This will revolutionize your business!")
- Be specific about timelines (90 days to results, not "immediate impact")
- Call out common objections directly

### What to AVOID

❌ **Technical jargon:**
- "API integration" → "connects with your existing software"
- "Python script" → "automated system"
- "Machine learning model" → "intelligent assessment"
- "Cron job at 5 PM" → "runs automatically every evening"

❌ **Vague claims:**
- "Significant improvement" → "90%+ compliance rate"
- "Saves time" → "5+ hours reclaimed per week"
- "Better results" → "eliminated six figures in write-offs"

❌ **Corporate speak:**
- "Leverage our platform" → "use the system"
- "Synergize workflows" → "connect existing tools"
- "Deliver value-add" → "get results"

❌ **Overselling:**
- "Revolutionary AI-powered solution"
- "Game-changing transformation"
- "Unlock unlimited potential"

## Content Structure

### Opening Hook (150-200 words)
**Purpose:** Get them nodding. "This is my problem too."

**Formula:**
1. Start with the dollar amount or time waste
2. Describe the daily operational pain (specific, relatable)
3. Why previous solutions failed
4. Tease the outcome without giving it away

**Example:**
> When a mid-sized service company in the Greater Toronto Area discovered they were writing off six figures annually due to poor technician documentation, they knew they had a problem. But like most service businesses with field teams, they didn't know how widespread it was until they ran the numbers.
>
> Incomplete work orders. Missing materials lists. No record of who signed off on the job. When clients disputed charges, the company had no documentation to back up their invoices. They'd either write off the charge or damage the client relationship by fighting over it.
>
> The finance team was frustrated. The operations manager was spending hours every week trying to track down information. And the technicians, who were great at their jobs, just didn't see documentation as a priority.

### The Problem Section (800-1,000 words)

**What to include:**
- **The Daily Pattern** - What's actually happening day-to-day
- **The Financial Impact** - Quantified pain (ranges, not exact numbers)
- **Why Previous Solutions Failed** - Training, manual spot-checks, performance metrics
- **The Breaking Point** - The moment they decided they had to fix this

**Structure:**
```
## The Problem: [Descriptive Title That Includes Keywords]

### The [Operational Issue] Spiral
- Daily pattern (bullet list)
- Financial impact (ranges, categories)
- Specific example (one client, one incident)

### Why Traditional Solutions Weren't Working
- Attempt #1: [Method] - Result: [Why it failed]
- Attempt #2: [Method] - Result: [Why it failed]
- Attempt #3: [Method] - Result: [Why it failed]

Core issue wasn't [surface problem], it was:
1. [Root cause 1]
2. [Root cause 2]
3. [Root cause 3]

### The Breaking Point
- The moment of realization
- What they needed (clear list)
```

**Tone notes:**
- Be empathetic (they're not incompetent, the problem is hard)
- Show you understand the constraints (can't just "hire more people")
- Use their language (if they call it "write-offs", you call it "write-offs")

### The Solution Section (600-800 words)

**❌ DON'T focus on:**
- Technical architecture
- Specific platforms or tools
- Implementation details
- Code or APIs

**✅ DO focus on:**
- **Create Visibility** - How the problem became visible
- **Build Accountability** - How feedback became immediate and specific
- **Align Incentives** - How behavior change was encouraged
- **Reduce Friction** - How it fit into existing workflows

**Structure:**
```
## The Solution: [Approach, Not Technology]

### Create Visibility
What data was surfaced, when, and how

### Build Accountability
How feedback was delivered (immediate, specific, actionable)

### Align Incentives
How the bonus/reward structure worked
Why team-based vs. individual

### Reduce Management Overhead
How it freed up the ops manager's time
```

**Language patterns:**
- "The system reviews each work order..." (not "A Python script pulls from the API...")
- "Technicians get specific feedback..." (not "Slack notifications via webhook...")
- "Creates immediate accountability..." (not "Automated compliance scoring...")

**Example (RIGHT):**
> We built a system that creates visibility, accountability, and incentive alignment around documentation quality.
>
> **Create Visibility:** Every evening, the system reviews each work order completed that day and scores it against a compliance checklist: time stamps logged, contact person documented, work performed described, materials used listed, customer sign-off captured.
>
> **Build Accountability:** Technicians with incomplete notes get immediate, specific feedback on what's missing and how to fix it. Not vague "document better" conversations. Just: "Job #4721 is missing materials used—here's the link to add it."

### The Results Section (400-600 words)

**Metrics to include:**
- Primary outcome (write-offs eliminated, compliance rate, time saved)
- Secondary outcomes (unexpected benefits)
- Timeline (how long to see results)
- Sustained results (not just a one-time spike)

**Structure:**
```
## Results in [Timeframe]

### By the Numbers
- Financial impact (ranges, percentages)
- Operational impact (hours saved, efficiency gained)
- Team impact (behavior changes, onboarding speed)

### The [30/60/90-Day] Timeline
- Weeks 1-3: Implementation phase
- Weeks 4-6: Soft launch (feedback only)
- Weeks 7-12: Full launch (with incentives)
- Month 4+: Sustained results

### What the Client Said
[Quote from client - even if anonymized]
Focus on unexpected benefits or mindset shifts
```

**Tone notes:**
- Lead with the big number (six figures, 90%+ compliance)
- Use percentages for improvements (~100% elimination, 90%+ rate)
- Don't make up quotes—use real client feedback or paraphrase with permission

### Why This Matters Section (400-500 words)

**Purpose:** Connect this specific problem to broader industry challenges

**Structure:**
```
## Why This Matters for [Industry Type]

### [Problem Type] Is a Hidden Tax on Growth
- Industry-wide statistics (2-5% revenue loss is common)
- Why it compounds as you scale
- Impact beyond just money

### The Real Cost Isn't Just Financial
- Customer relationship damage
- Employee frustration
- Slower cash flow
- Inability to scale

### Why Systems Beat Training Every Time
- Training = tells people what to do
- Systems = ensure it actually happens
- The tool is irrelevant, principles matter
```

**Key message:**
"This isn't about technology. It's about visibility, accountability, and incentives."

### Key Takeaways Section

**Format:** 5-6 bullet points

**What to include:**
- Principle-based insights (not tactical steps)
- Applicable to readers' businesses
- Focus on mindset shifts and approach

**Examples:**
- "Create visibility into the problem - you can't fix what you can't see"
- "Make feedback immediate and specific - not delayed and vague"
- "Align incentives with outcomes - reward what you want to see more of"
- "Use team-based (not individual) incentives to build peer accountability"
- "The tool doesn't matter - visibility, accountability, and incentives do"

### FAQ Section (500-700 words)

**Purpose:** Answer objections and capture featured snippets

**Questions to include:**
- How long does implementation take?
- What if we use [different tool]?
- Does this work with our software?
- Will our team feel micromanaged?
- What if [specific concern]?
- Can we pilot with one team first?
- What happens after implementation?

**❌ DON'T include:**
- Pricing questions (remove entirely)
- Technical questions about implementation
- Questions that position you as vendor

**✅ DO include:**
- Change management questions
- ROI and timeline questions
- Team adoption questions
- Sustainability questions

**Format:**
- H3 heading as question
- 75-100 word answer
- Specific, not vague
- End with benefit, not feature

**Example:**
> ### Will technicians feel micromanaged?
>
> This was the operations manager's biggest concern too. In practice, the opposite happened. Technicians prefer immediate, clear feedback over vague 'you need to document better' conversations in performance reviews. The key is making feedback specific and actionable, not punitive.

## SEO Guidelines

### URL Structure
`/case-studies/[problem-focused-keyword]`

Examples:
- ✅ `/case-studies/eliminate-invoice-writeoffs-service-business`
- ✅ `/case-studies/reduce-customer-churn-msp`
- ❌ `/case-studies/technician-note-compliance` (feature-focused)
- ❌ `/case-studies/client-x-success-story` (not keyword-focused)

### Title & Meta Description

**Title Format (60-68 chars):**
"How to [Solve Problem] in [Industry] | Toronto Case Study"

**Meta Description (155-160 chars):**
"See how a [location]-based [business type] [achieved outcome] by [approach]. Real metrics, real results."

**Examples:**
- Title: "How to Eliminate Invoice Write-Offs in Service Businesses | Toronto Case Study"
- Meta: "See how a GTA-based service company with 30+ field technicians eliminated six figures in invoice write-offs by creating visibility, accountability, and aligned incentives."

### Keyword Strategy

**Primary keyword:** The problem being solved
- "eliminate invoice write-offs"
- "reduce customer churn"
- "improve field documentation"

**Secondary keywords:** Variations and related searches
- "billing disputes service business"
- "field technician documentation"
- "service company revenue loss"

**Geographic keywords:** Location signals (4-6 mentions naturally throughout)
- Toronto service company
- GTA-based business
- Ontario service business

**Long-tail keywords:** More specific, less competitive
- "how to stop invoice write-offs service business"
- "field service documentation best practices toronto"

### Content Optimization

**Word count:** 2,500-3,000 words (signals comprehensive content)

**Heading hierarchy:**
- H1: Main title (one per page)
- H2: Major sections
- H3: Subsections and FAQ questions

**Internal linking:**
- Link to 4-6 related pages (other case studies, services, about page)
- Use descriptive anchor text ("learn more about our approach to automation")

**External linking:**
- 2-3 links to authoritative sources (industry associations, research)
- Builds trust and context

## Writing Process

### Step 1: Client Interview (60-90 minutes)

**Questions to ask:**

**About the Problem:**
1. What was the financial impact? (dollar amount or range)
2. How much time was being wasted? (hours per week/month)
3. What had they tried before that didn't work?
4. What was the "breaking point" moment?
5. What were they most worried about?

**About the Solution:**
1. How does the system work (in business terms, not technical)?
2. What was the team's reaction during rollout?
3. What was surprising or unexpected?
4. How did behavior actually change?
5. How did management's role shift?

**About the Results:**
1. What metrics improved (be specific)?
2. How long until they saw results?
3. What secondary benefits emerged (unplanned outcomes)?
4. What would they say to another business owner considering this?
5. What would have happened if they didn't do this?

**Get their actual words:**
- Record the call (with permission)
- Use their language in the case study
- Their credibility > your eloquence

### Step 2: Outline (1-2 hours)

1. Open your template (from this guide)
2. Fill in bullet points for each section
3. Map where keywords will appear naturally
4. Note where client quotes will go
5. Identify any missing information (follow up with client)

### Step 3: First Draft (6-8 hours)

**Focus on getting words on the page. Don't edit yet.**

**Writing order:**
1. Problem section (nail the pain)
2. Solution section (principles, not tech)
3. Results section (numbers and timeline)
4. Why This Matters (broader context)
5. Key Takeaways (distill insights)
6. FAQ (answer objections)
7. Opening hook (write last, after you know the story)

**Tips:**
- Write in one sitting if possible
- Let the first draft be messy
- Use client's actual words for quotes
- Add brackets for [NEED TO VERIFY] items

### Step 4: Edit for Principles (2-3 hours)

**Search your draft for these red flags:**

❌ Technical terms:
- API, Python, GitHub Actions, cron jobs, webhooks
- Replace with business outcomes

❌ Tool/platform names:
- "Claude API" → "intelligent assessment"
- "Slack notifications" → "immediate feedback"

❌ Vague claims:
- "significant improvement" → "90%+ compliance"
- "faster" → "5+ hours saved per week"

❌ Corporate speak:
- "leverage" → "use"
- "synergize" → "connect"
- "deliver value-add" → "get results"

**Ask yourself:**
1. Would a skeptical business owner believe this?
2. Did I focus on behavior change, not technology?
3. Are the numbers specific but not fabricated?
4. Did I remove ALL pricing references?
5. Would my 16-year-old nephew understand this?

### Step 5: SEO Optimization (1-2 hours)

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Primary keyword in H1, first 100 words, 2-3x throughout
- [ ] Secondary keywords in H2/H3 tags
- [ ] Geographic terms mentioned 4-6x naturally
- [ ] Meta title (60-68 chars)
- [ ] Meta description (155-160 chars)
- [ ] 4-6 internal links added
- [ ] 2-3 external authority links added
- [ ] FAQ questions formatted as H3 (for featured snippets)

### Step 6: Client Review (1-2 hours)

**Send to client with clear ask:**

> "I've drafted a case study based on our conversation. Two things I need from you:
>
> 1. **Fact-check the numbers** - Are the metrics accurate?
> 2. **Permission to publish** - Even though we've anonymized your company name, I need written approval to publish this.
>
> If anything feels off or you'd prefer different phrasing, let me know."

**Incorporate feedback:**
- Don't argue with their corrections
- If they want something removed, remove it
- Get written approval before publishing

### Step 7: Publish & Promote (2-3 hours)

**Before publishing:**
- Final proofread (Grammarly)
- Check all links work
- Verify mobile responsive
- Test page load speed

**After publishing:**
- Submit to Google Search Console
- Share on LinkedIn with key stat
- Email to newsletter subscribers
- Post in relevant industry forums/groups
- Add to proposals and email signatures

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### ❌ Mistake #1: Getting Too Technical
**Wrong:** "We built a Python script that calls the Claude API to parse technician notes using NLP..."

**Right:** "The system reviews each work order and assesses documentation quality against a clear checklist..."

### ❌ Mistake #2: Burying the Outcome
**Wrong:** Waiting until the end to reveal results

**Right:** Tease the outcome in the opening paragraph, detail it later

### ❌ Mistake #3: Vague Social Proof
**Wrong:** "The client was very happy with the results."

**Right:** "The operations manager said: 'I thought technicians would hate getting feedback. Instead, they asked why we didn't do this sooner.'"

### ❌ Mistake #4: Overselling
**Wrong:** "This revolutionary AI-powered system will transform your business overnight!"

**Right:** "Over 90 days, compliance improved from 58% to 90%+, eliminating six figures in write-offs."

### ❌ Mistake #5: Mentioning Pricing
**Wrong:** "The implementation cost $12K but paid for itself in 30 days."

**Right:** "The ROI was clear within the first month as write-offs dropped significantly."

## Questions or Exceptions?

**"What if the client wants to talk about the technology?"**
Include a brief technical appendix at the end, but keep the main case study focused on business outcomes.

**"What if the numbers are too vague?"**
Ranges are fine. "Six figures", "90%+", "5-10 hours" are all specific enough to be credible.

**"What if the client wants pricing included?"**
Politely decline. Pricing varies by complexity and scope. Direct them to book a discovery call instead.

**"What if I can't verify the numbers?"**
Use conservative estimates or remove the metric entirely. Credibility > impressive-sounding claims.

## Final Word

Every case study should answer three questions:
1. **Did they have a problem I recognize?** (The Problem section)
2. **Did you actually solve it?** (The Results section)
3. **Could this work for me?** (Why This Matters section)

If you can answer all three, the case study works. If not, keep editing.

Write for the skeptical business owner who's been burned before. Make them believe you understand their problem, and that you've solved it for others just like them.

**The best case studies don't sound like marketing. They sound like honest conversations about hard problems and how they got solved.**
